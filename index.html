The Problem

A Growing Crisis at the Earliest Intervention Point

Suicide remains a significant public health issue in ASEAN, particularly among youth — accounting for 1.1% of global deaths, with 73% occurring in low- and middle-income countries. While overall rates in the WHO South-East Asia region declined by 26% (2000–2021), youth suicide (ages 5–19) continues to rise, with 6–16% of young adults reporting suicidal ideation.

Risk intensifies during adolescence — particularly ages 13–18 — where academic pressure, identity formation, and emotional instability converge. These risks are driven by overlapping factors: depression, anxiety, bullying, family conflict, and academic pressure — all of which first manifest in the home environment yet are frequently missed or misunderstood.

This creates a breakdown at the earliest possible intervention point. Parents, positioned as the first line of response, often lack the communication skills to recognise warning signs. Without early action, risks escalate quickly and become significantly harder to reverse.

Target Groups

Who We're Reaching & Why

Primary Target

👨‍👩‍👧 Parents of High School Students

Chosen as the highest-leverage intervention point during peak risk years (13–18). Most warning signs first appear at home, yet parents often lack the skills to recognise distress or communicate without conflict. Targeting parents directly closes this early-detection gap where intervention is still possible — before behaviours escalate beyond family reach.
Secondary Target

🎒 High School Students (13–18)

Not directly targeted due to access constraints (safeguarding, school permissions) and lower feasibility within the timeline. Influence is instead applied indirectly through parents, who shape daily environment, communication patterns, and early response. Risk escalates earlier than university, with sharp increases already visible at 13–18 — making parent-based intervention the most feasible and effective pathway.

Strategy: Intervene where risk is high and control is still possible — parents (high influence, accessible) → students (high risk, limited direct access).

Proposed Solution

A Demand-Driven, Schools-First Programme

Eight sequenced activities target behaviour change — not only awareness. Parents initiate conversations earlier, recognise warning signs, and take action. Students benefit indirectly through improved home environments.

Risk Management

Key Risks & Mitigations

Parents
Low survey response / low engagement
Use school channels + trusted messaging; keep survey short and accessible; frame around children's wellbeing.
Parents
Low workshop attendance
Schedule at evenings/weekends; send school reminders; incentivise with free Connection Kit for attendees.
Parents
Stigma around mental health
Use non-clinical, neutral language; frame as practical parenting support — "communication skills," not "mental illness."
Parents
Lack of trust in student-led project
Include expert validation and psychologist guest speakers; present credentials prominently.
Students
Low engagement & resistance
Gamification + ice-breaking activities; peer-to-peer facilitators; collaborate with school clubs for peer encouragement.
Students
Psychological triggers during sessions
Ensure a professional counsellor is on-site; provide a designated quiet/safe space; have crisis protocol ready.
Students
Intergenerational conflict
Use breakout rooms — separate sessions for parents and students — allowing both to be heard under psychologist guidance.
Students
Lack of long-term follow-up
Create a resource toolkit with psychologist contacts; build a clear referral pathway for ongoing support after the programme.
