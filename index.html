Section 01

Stakeholder Collaboration

Structured collaboration — with clear methods, defined roles, and explicit accountability — is what separates a project that funders trust from one that sounds well-intentioned but undeliverable. Every stakeholder relationship must answer: what do they contribute, how do we engage them, and what does success look like?

Why Structure Matters

Credibility is built through clarity

Funders, schools, and parents all ask the same silent question: "Can this team actually deliver?" A clear stakeholder map with defined engagement methods answers that question before it's asked. It signals that the team has thought beyond the idea to the reality of execution.

Why Responsibility Matters

Accountability drives follow-through

When each stakeholder relationship has a named owner and an explicit engagement method, responsibility doesn't diffuse across the team. It sticks. This prevents the most common failure mode of youth-led projects: good intentions without consistent follow-up.

🎯
One owner per relationship
Every external stakeholder relationship has a single named team member responsible for maintaining contact. Split ownership leads to no ownership — and missed follow-ups are the #1 reason school partnerships fall through.
📋
Structured method, not just goodwill
Every collaboration uses a defined method: formal letters for schools (not just verbal agreements), written mentor feedback (not just verbal discussion), MOUs for psychologist partnerships. Structure creates the paper trail funders need to see.
🔁
Feedback flows in both directions
Internal: every team meeting closes with a structured reflection. External: every workshop ends with a short feedback form. This data feeds directly into KPI measurement and pitch evidence — nothing is anecdotal.
Section 02

Marketing Communication Strategy

The two approaches require distinct communication strategies — because the audiences, channels, tone, and goals are fundamentally different. A message that resonates with a worried parent will not land with a 16-year-old on TikTok, and vice versa.

Approach 1 · Parents

Authority, Warmth & Practical Tools

Parents need to feel that this is credible, trustworthy, and actionable — not alarmist or clinical. The tone is warm authority. The goal is to reduce their anxiety about the topic by giving them concrete tools.

🏫
School Channels (Primary)
Official letters via school, parent WhatsApp groups, parent-teacher meeting announcements. Leverages institutional trust — parents engage because the school sent it.
📦
Connection Kit (Core Asset)
Physical/digital kit with conversation starter cards, warning signs reference, and helpline QR code. Warm, non-clinical design. Distributed at school or workshop — tangible proof of the project.
💻
Online Workshops ×3
Monthly sessions covering: understanding teen mental health → having the conversation → when and how to seek professional help. Progressive structure builds parent confidence over time.
📲
Social Media (Facebook / Zalo)
Workshop announcements, myth-busting posts, parent testimonials (anonymised). Platform choice matches where Vietnamese parents are — not TikTok.
Core Message to Parents

"You don't need to be a psychologist to help your child. You just need to know how to start the conversation — and we'll show you how."

Approach 2 · Students

Peer Voice, Normalisation & Zero Pressure

Students are allergic to being talked at. The communication must feel peer-originated — relatable, low-pressure, and non-clinical. The goal is not to diagnose or intervene, but to normalise and signpost.

🎬
Short-Form Video (TikTok / Reels)
2–3 videos per week. Relatable scenarios, myth-busting, peer stories. No clinical language. Format-first thinking: content must feel native to the platform, not like a PSA.
🖼️
School Posters & Environment
Placed in classrooms, hallways, and bathrooms. Youth-friendly visuals. QR code to resources. The poster is a passive, low-stigma touchpoint — students see it without being singled out.
🎤
In-School Session (45 min × 3 schools)
Facilitated discussion — not a lecture. Anonymous Q&A, peer scenarios, storytelling. One psychologist present for credibility. Goal: reduce stigma and introduce available resources.
🃏
Peer Connection Card
Pocket-sized card with: a validating message, 2–3 local helplines, a prompt to talk to someone. Distributed at session. Mirrors the parent kit but in youth language.
Core Message to Students

"Feeling overwhelmed doesn't make you weak. It makes you human. And there are people — closer than you think — who want to help."

How We Measure

Measurement Framework

Six measurement tools ensure that every KPI has a collection method — not just a target. Data collection is built into delivery, not added afterwards.

Tool 01
Pre/Post Survey (Parents)
Sent before Workshop 1 and after Workshop 3. Measures confidence change, awareness shift, and self-reported behaviour change. Owner: Phượng.
Tool 02
Pre/Post Survey (Students)
Anonymous 5-question survey administered at each in-school session — before and after. Measures stigma reduction and awareness improvement. Owner: Trí.
Tool 03
Social Media Analytics
Weekly screenshot log of impressions, reach, and engagement rate per post. Compiled into a running tracker. Owner: Hà.
Tool 04
Workshop Attendance Log
Registration form (Google Form) for all 3 workshops. Cross-referenced with kit distribution. Owner: Linh.
Tool 05
Partnership Confirmation Record
Folder of signed MOUs, confirmation emails, and pro-bono agreements. Presented as an appendix in the pitch deck. Owner: Tiên.
Tool 06
KPI Dashboard (Running)
A single shared tracking sheet updated weekly. Red/amber/green status for each KPI. Reviewed at every team check-in. Owner: Phượng.
