The Problem

A Growing Crisis Across ASEAN Youth

Suicide remains a significant public health issue across ASEAN, particularly among youth — accounting for 1.1% of global deaths, with 73% occurring in low- and middle-income countries. While overall rates in the WHO South-East Asia region declined by 26% between 2000–2021, youth suicide (ages 5–19) continues to rise, with 6–16% of young adults reporting suicidal ideation. Risk intensifies sharply during adolescence, particularly between ages 13–18, where academic pressure, identity formation, and emotional instability converge. Early warning signs appear in daily home interactions — yet are consistently missed, leaving adolescents without timely support during the most critical intervention window.

🎯 Definite Project Goal

Within the 6-month programme (May–November 2026), equip 200+ parents of high school students with practical communication tools to recognise and respond to early mental health distress in their children — through a demand-driven survey, 3 expert-led workshops, and the Connection Kit — while achieving indirect reach to 500+ students across 3+ partner schools.

⚠️ Why not target students directly? High-school students face more structured pressure and have less autonomy than university students. Access constraints (safeguarding, school permissions) and low feasibility within a short timeline make direct engagement impractical. Influence is applied indirectly through parents — who shape the daily environment, communication patterns, and early response during the 13–18 window.

Strategic Rationale

Why Two Approaches?

👨‍👩‍👧

Parents

Primary Target

The highest-leverage intervention point during peak risk years (13–18). Warning signs first appear at home, yet parents often lack the skills to recognise distress or communicate without conflict. Targeting parents directly closes the early-detection gap while intervention is still possible. Schools provide credible, trusted access to this group.

🎒

Students

Secondary Target (Indirect)

The direct beneficiaries, reached indirectly through improved parenting. Risk escalates fastest between 13–18, with grades, parental expectations, and career decisions converging before adolescents have developed strong coping mechanisms. Influence flows through parents who shape the home environment and early response — the most feasible and effective pathway.

The sequencing matters: schools → parents → students. Intervene where risk is high and control is still possible — using parents as the primary lever to reach students during the critical 13–18 window.

Approach 1
Parents

Empowering the First Responders at Home

The project targets behaviour change, not only awareness — parents initiating conversations earlier, recognising warning signs, and taking action to support or seek help. Every activity is demand-driven and expert-validated.

1
School Partnership & Outreach

Partner with 3+ high schools to distribute materials and host sessions via existing parent channels (meetings, emails, WhatsApp groups). Schools add institutional credibility — parents engage because the school endorsed it. Start before the new school year begins to minimise study-load conflicts.

2
Demand-Driven Parent Survey (Month 1)

Distribute a survey to parents via school channels to let them select what they want to learn from professionals (e.g. warning signs, causes, communication strategies). Target 70–150 responses per school. The top 3 selected topics drive all downstream content — ensuring high relevance and demand-led delivery rather than generic programming.

3
Connection Kit — Core Deliverable (Month 2)

A practical, non-clinical toolkit distributed to 200+ parents: conversation starter cards, warning-signs reference sheet, helpline QR code, and visual reminders. Translates expert knowledge into daily-use tools. Warm, approachable design — never medical or alarmist.

4
Three Expert-Led Online Workshops (Months 3–5)

Content built around the top 3 parent-selected topics. Each workshop includes live Q&A with a mental health professional.
Workshop 1: Understanding Your Teenager's Inner World (Month 3)
Workshop 2: Opening the Conversation — scripts & open dialogue Q&A (Month 4)
Workshop 3: When to Seek Help & How — navigating the support system (Month 5)
Target: ≥70% attendance rate (70–80 parents per school per session).

5
Follow-Up, Reinforcement & Evaluation (Month 6)

Post-workshop materials (summaries, key messages, visual reminders) reinforce learning beyond the sessions. Feedback forms collected after each workshop (target ≥60% completion rate). Certificate issued to parents who attend all 3 workshops. Evaluation tracks whether ≥30% of parents report applying new communication or recognition skills at home.

Approach 2
Students

Indirect Impact Through a Stronger Home Environment

Students are reached indirectly through parents, but also through school-based touchpoints that normalise help-seeking. Direct access is limited by safeguarding and timeline constraints — but environmental and peer-driven nudges create meaningful change.

1
In-School Survey (Parallel to Parent Survey)

An anonymous in-school survey to understand the current state of student mental health, identify whether issues are in "prevention" or "crisis" mode, and surface the top 3 most pressing concerns. Data tailors campaign messaging and informs future workshop planning.

2
School-Based Awareness Campaign

Posters in classrooms, hallways, and bathrooms using youth-friendly language and visuals with QR codes to helplines. Passive, low-stigma touchpoint — students absorb without being singled out. A peer-led feel, not top-down messaging.

3
In-School Workshops / Talkshows & Safe Sharing Sessions

Facilitated open-dialogue sessions (not lectures) with peer scenarios, anonymous Q&A, and one psychologist present. Gamification and ice-breaking activities help students open up. Peer-to-peer facilitators (young mentors) make sharing easier. One session per partner school — 45 minutes minimum.

4
TikTok / Instagram Reels Content Series

2+ posts per week. Relatable peer-voice scenarios, "Truth Bomb" myth-busting, and storytelling — content must feel native to the platform, not like a public service announcement. No clinical language. Extends reach beyond the 3 partner schools at minimal additional cost.

5
Peer Connection Card

A pocket-sized card distributed at each in-school session: a validating message, 2–3 helpline numbers, and a prompt to talk to someone. Written in Gen Z / Gen Alpha language. Mirrors the parent kit but built for students. Provides immediate resource signposting after each touchpoint.

Risk Management

Key Risks & Mitigations

⚠️ Low Parent Survey Response / Engagement
Use school channels and trusted messaging to draw attention to children's mental health issues. Keep the survey short and easy to understand. Frame as practical parenting support — not a mental health intervention. Use non-clinical, neutral language; focus messaging on "supporting children."
⚠️ Low Workshop Attendance or Drop-Off
Schedule at evenings and weekends. Send reminders via schools. Deliver clear value per session with simple takeaways. Avoid blame — use general scenarios, anonymised examples, and self-reflection framing. Incentivise full attendance with a certificate issued to parents who complete all 3 sessions.
⚠️ Student Resistance / Psychological Triggers
Use gamification and ice-breaking activities. Deploy peer-to-peer facilitators (young mentors) to reduce barriers. Ensure a professional counsellor is on-site to handle emotional responses. Provide a quiet room / safe space to promote a sense of security during sessions.
⚠️ Intergenerational Conflict in Sessions
Use breakout rooms separating parent and student sessions so both feel heard without pressure. Guide both groups toward understanding each other's perspectives under psychologist facilitation — preventing parents from defaulting into "teaching mode" and students from feeling dismissed.
⚠️ Lack of Trust in Student-Led Project
Include expert validation through guest speaker psychologists in all workshops and in-school sessions. MOUs with schools and formal pro-bono agreements with professionals serve as credibility anchors for funders and participants alike.
⚠️ Limited Access / No Long-Term Follow-Up
Create a resource toolkit with guidance and psychologist contact information for ongoing support. Hybrid model (online + offline) accommodates students with disabilities or access constraints. Reinforce learning through follow-up materials beyond each session.

Section 01

Stakeholder Collaboration

Structured collaboration — with clear methods, defined roles, and explicit accountability — is what separates a project that funders trust from one that sounds well-intentioned but undeliverable. Every stakeholder relationship must answer: what do they contribute, how do we engage them, and what does success look like?

Why Structure Matters

Credibility is built through clarity

Funders, schools, and parents all ask the same silent question: "Can this team actually deliver?" A clear stakeholder map with defined engagement methods answers that question before it's asked. It signals that the team has thought beyond the idea to the reality of execution.

Why Responsibility Matters

Accountability drives follow-through

When each stakeholder relationship has a named owner and an explicit engagement method, responsibility doesn't diffuse across the team. It sticks. This prevents the most common failure mode of youth-led projects: good intentions without consistent follow-up.

Section 02

Marketing Communication Strategy

The two approaches require distinct communication strategies — because the audiences, channels, tone, and goals are fundamentally different. A message that resonates with a worried parent will not land with a 16-year-old on TikTok, and vice versa.

Approach 1 · Parents

Authority, Warmth & Practical Tools

Parents need to feel that this is credible, trustworthy, and actionable — not alarmist or clinical. The tone is warm authority. The goal is to reduce their anxiety about the topic by giving them concrete tools.

🏫
School Channels (Primary)
Official letters via school, parent WhatsApp groups, parent-teacher meeting announcements. Leverages institutional trust — parents engage because the school sent it.
📦
Connection Kit (Core Asset)
Physical/digital kit with conversation starter cards, warning signs reference, and helpline QR code. Warm, non-clinical design. Distributed at school or workshop — tangible proof of the project.
💻
Online Workshops ×3
Monthly sessions covering: understanding teen mental health → having the conversation → when and how to seek professional help. Progressive structure builds parent confidence over time.
📲
Social Media (Facebook / Zalo)
Workshop announcements, myth-busting posts, parent testimonials (anonymised). Platform choice matches where Vietnamese parents are — not TikTok.
Core Message to Parents

"You don't need to be a psychologist to help your child. You just need to know how to start the conversation — and we'll show you how."

Approach 2 · Students

Peer Voice, Normalisation & Zero Pressure

Students are allergic to being talked at. The communication must feel peer-originated — relatable, low-pressure, and non-clinical. The goal is not to diagnose or intervene, but to normalise and signpost.

🎬
Short-Form Video (TikTok / Reels)
2–3 videos per week. Relatable scenarios, myth-busting, peer stories. No clinical language. Format-first thinking: content must feel native to the platform, not like a PSA.
🖼️
School Posters & Environment
Placed in classrooms, hallways, and bathrooms. Youth-friendly visuals. QR code to resources. The poster is a passive, low-stigma touchpoint — students see it without being singled out.
🎤
In-School Session (45 min × 3 schools)
Facilitated discussion — not a lecture. Anonymous Q&A, peer scenarios, storytelling. One psychologist present for credibility. Goal: reduce stigma and introduce available resources.
🃏
Peer Connection Card
Pocket-sized card with: a validating message, 2–3 local helplines, a prompt to talk to someone. Distributed at session. Mirrors the parent kit but in youth language.
Core Message to Students

"Feeling overwhelmed doesn't make you weak. It makes you human. And there are people — closer than you think — who want to help."

How We Measure

Measurement Framework

Six measurement tools ensure that every KPI has a collection method — not just a target. Data collection is built into delivery, not added afterwards.

Tool 01
Pre/Post Survey (Parents)
Sent before Workshop 1 and after Workshop 3. Measures confidence change, awareness shift, and self-reported behaviour change. Owner: Phượng.
Tool 02
Pre/Post Survey (Students)
Anonymous 5-question survey administered at each in-school session — before and after. Measures stigma reduction and awareness improvement. Owner: Trí.
Tool 03
Social Media Analytics
Weekly screenshot log of impressions, reach, and engagement rate per post. Compiled into a running tracker. Owner: Hà.
Tool 04
Workshop Attendance Log
Registration form (Google Form) for all 3 workshops. Cross-referenced with kit distribution. Owner: Linh.
Tool 05
Partnership Confirmation Record
Folder of signed MOUs, confirmation emails, and pro-bono agreements. Presented as an appendix in the pitch deck. Owner: Tiên.
Tool 06
KPI Dashboard (Running)
A single shared tracking sheet updated weekly. Red/amber/green status for each KPI. Reviewed at every team check-in. Owner: Phượng.
Volunteer Strategy

How Volunteers Power This Project

Bridging the Silence is itself a volunteer-led initiative. The six team members are not just organisers — they are the volunteers. This section applies the DSC 3-Step framework and NCVO standards to define how volunteering is structured, supported, and made meaningful within the project.

Step 1

Mission Alignment

How do volunteers support the mission, vision & goals?

Volunteers directly deliver the project's two approaches. Parents team (Linh, C.Thy, Phượng) drives family engagement; Students team (Trí, Hà, Tiên) drives school-level change. Every volunteer hour maps to a measurable outcome.

Step 2

Supporting Volunteers

How will the organisation support volunteers to achieve this?

Weekly check-ins led by team leader Tien. Clear task ownership with deadlines. Mentor sessions on 7 & 21 April provide external guidance. Feedback culture built into every team meeting.

Step 3

Promoting Volunteering

How will volunteering be promoted internally & externally?

Team members model the project's values publicly. Social media content features their voices. The pitch itself showcases volunteering as the engine of the project — demonstrating impact to funders and future recruits.

Volunteering Culture

Building a Pro-Volunteering Culture

The DSC framework warns that "culture eats strategy for breakfast." For Bridging the Silence, the team is small and tight-knit — which is an advantage. But culture still needs to be intentional.

✅ What makes this team's culture strong
  • · Clear mission everyone believes in
  • · Divided ownership — no role overlap or ambiguity
  • · Senior support via mentor sessions
  • · Short, fixed timeline reduces burnout risk
  • · Each member develops real, named skills
⚠️ Culture risks to manage
  • · Unequal workload across the 6 members
  • · Communication gaps between the two approach teams
  • · No formal recognition system for effort
  • · Volunteers may feel undervalued without feedback
  • · Tight deadline = stress without check-in structure

Recognition & Wellbeing

Keeping Volunteers Motivated

Volunteero and NCVO both emphasise that recognition is not optional — it is what separates a project that retains its people from one that burns them out. For a 6-week sprint, simple and consistent beats elaborate.

📣
Public Recognition

Name each team member in the final pitch deck and all project materials. Their contributions are visible to funders.

🔁
Feedback Loop

End each team meeting with a 5-minute reflection: what went well, what was hard. Log it. Use it in the final self-appraisal.

🎓
Skills Certificate

Issue each team member a record of the 4 skills developed: communication, teamwork, self-appraisal, sales & pitching.

💰 Volunteer Resourcing Note (for the pitch)

NCVO requires that volunteering is reflected in financial planning. For the pitch, state explicitly: the 6-member team contributes an estimated X hours each across the project period — representing a combined volunteer contribution of approximately [total hours] hours of unpaid skilled work. Quantifying this as an in-kind contribution strengthens the funding case by showing the project's true cost and the team's commitment.
